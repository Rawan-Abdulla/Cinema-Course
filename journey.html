Egypt

It is well known that Egypt is the largest film producer in the Middle East. Egypt's history of films began a few months after the" Lumiere Brothers "was shown in Europe. In 1896, their film was transferred to Egypt and showed exclusively to a group of Egyptians.
Egypt was one of the few countries in the Arab world and the Middle East that managed to establish a film industry during its colonization. However, the types of films shot in Egypt at that time were more documentary, similar to cinema or news films.
Also, Egypt made more news releases and also started making short films, "from 1927 to 1930 two full films were produced per year, five films were released in 1931 and six films each in 1933 and 1934".

Although all of these films were created independently, in 1935 Studio (مصر) was established and followed by six other studios from 1936 to 1984. Egypt was the most powerful Arab and Middle Eastern country to produce films, which many other countries in the Middle East were looking forward to being like it.
Over time, many changes took place that prompted many Egyptian filmmakers to gradually start producing their films in Lebanon. First, the Egyptian Revolution of 1952 made it less politically stable in Egypt. Second, the nationalization of the film industry (1962) in Egypt led to more restrictions on films slowly being imposed until the film censorship laws issued in 1976.

Although the Egyptian film industry is not as strong as it was before due to the growing financial problems, it still dominates Arab cinema output, especially in the mainstream cinema, where it produces up to 77% of the films. This is because there is already a system in place for Egyptian cinema which saw its rise in the 1960s.

Syria

In 1928, the first Syrian feature film (The Innocent Victim) was produced based on real events, and despite its commercial success, the Syrian film industry began to diminish due to the huge diversification of Egyptian production.

After its establishment in 1958, the Ministry of Culture and Guidance was interested in establishing a cinema department with the aim of spreading cinematic culture, establishing a film library and producing cinematic films, and the Ministry contracted with Syrian and foreign experts who produced a large number of short films about Syria, its archeology, popular arts, and its economic and social development.

In 1963, the Syrian General Organization for Cinema began sending a number of young people abroad to learn cinematic art in the most famous film institutes in the world, and these young men returned from their missions to push cinematic art in their countries forward to start a new cinematic art that differs from what it was before.

By the 1970s, Syria was producing many high-quality documentaries and fictional films, as films such as The Knife, The Deceived, and Kafr Qasem, made Damascus the alternative focal point in the Arab film industry, but in the eighties, Syrian cinema became more associated with a specific group of directors such as Samir Zekri, Muhammad Malas, Osama Muhammad.

Palestine

The first Palestinian film to be made is generally believed to be a documentary on King Ibn Saud of Saudi Arabia's visit in 1935 to Palestine, made by Ibrahim Hassan Sirhan, based in Jaffa. Sirhan followed the King and around Palestine, "from Lod to Jaffa and from Jaffa to Tel Aviv". The result was a silent movie. In 1945 Sirhan established the "Arab Film Company". which was followed by preparations for the next film A Storm at Home. The films themselves were lost in 1948.

The 1948 Palestinian exodus had a devastating effect on the Palestinian society, including its nascent film industry. It is reported that Sirhan was involved with the production of the first Jordanian feature film, The Struggle in Jarash (1957), second Jordanian feature film, My Homeland, My Love, in 1964.

After 1967 Palestinian cinema was founded. More than 60 films were made in this period, mostly documentaries. The first film festival dedicated to Palestinian films was held in Baghdad in 1973. Mustafa Abu Ali was one of the early Palestinian film directors, and he helped found the Palestinian Cinema Association in Beirut in 1973.

The largest archive was run by PLO's Film Foundation/Palestinian Film Unit. In 1982, when the PLO was forced out of Beirut, the archive was in storage. it "disappeared" under circumstances that are still unclear.
The 1996 drama/comedy Chronicle of a Disappearance received international critical acclaim. and it became the first Palestinian movie to receive a national release in the United States. In 2008 try to reopen Cinema Jenin, a cinema located in the Jenin Refugee Camp. In 2010, Hamas, announced the completion of a new film. Titled The Great Liberation, the film depicts the destruction of Israel by Palestinians.

Iraq

Before the First World War, cinema did not have a presence in Iraq, and although the first film shown in Iraq was in 1909, no clear cinematic activity arose, as silent foreign films remained available until some merchants built cinemas in Baghdad and cities, and they started importing some of these films from time to time.

In the 1940s under the rule of King Faisal II of Iraq, a true Iraqi cinema began. The General Authority for Cinema and Theater was established in Iraq in 1959 by the left-leaning national government, where it began producing many documentaries, but with the outbreak of the Iran-Iraq war in the early 1980s, Iraqi cinema stopped.

Saddam Hussein’s arrival to power in 1979 pushed Iraqi cinema in a slightly different direction. The depletion of national resources from the 1980 Iran-Iraq War nearly halted film production. The few films produced were mainly aimed at glorifying legendary Iraqi history or celebrating Saddam Hussein’s rule. And then after Iraq began the attacks on Kuwait, Iraq's sanctions made filmmaking impossible in the country, despite the fact that a new generation of directors is pulsing.

The Arb Maghreb(Algeria, Morocco, and Tunisia)

National cinema appeared in the Arb Maghreb - Tunisia, Morocco, and Algeria - in the wake of the independence of these countries. In 1946, the French established huge studios in Tunisia and Morocco, and they did so to ensure a cinematic alternative in the Arabic language (with French colonial propaganda) to counter the popularity of Egyptian cinema. Which came out of these studios produced by foreigners.
After Algeria gained its independence in 1962, it began to focus its films mainly on topics related to the liberation war, such as the movie Battle of Algeria, which became one of the landmarks in the history of the so-called third cinema. In 1964, Algeria established the National Center for Cinema, where it produced many high-profile films such as Rih al-Aures(ريح الاوراس) by Muhammad Khidr Hamina, Opium and the stick (الافيون والعصا) by Ahmad Rachidi, Wind of the South( ريح الجنوب) by Mohamed Samir Riad, in addition to documentaries and short films. The film Chronique des années de braise "The Chronicle of the Years of Embers" is one of the five feature films made in the mid-1970s by Muhammad Khidr Hamina, which won the Grand Prix at Cannes in 1975.
In the early 1980s, Amin Mirbal and Muhammad Bou Ameri, along with many other filmmakers, began to focus on issues of agrarian reform, industrialization, and the status of migrant workers from North Africa in Europe.

Cinema in Morocco has a long history, stretching back over a century to the filming of Le chevrier Marocain ("The Moroccan Goatherd") by Louis Lumière in 1897. Between that time and 1944, many foreign movies were shot in the country, especially in the Ouarzazate area.

In 1944, the Moroccan Cinematographic Center (CCM), the nation's film regulatory body, was established. Studios were also opened in Rabat.

In 1952, Orson Welles' Othello won the Palme d'Or at the Cannes Film Festival under the Moroccan flag. However, the Festival's musicians did not play the Moroccan national anthem, as no one in attendance knew what it was. Six years later, Mohammed Ousfour would create the first Moroccan movie, Le fils maudit ("The Damned Son").

In 1968, the first Mediterranean Film Festival was held in Tangier.This was followed in 1982 with the first national festival of cinema, which was held in Rabat. In 2001, the first International Film Festival of Marrakech (FIFM) was also held in Marrakech.

In 1919, the first feature-length movie produced in North Africa: Les Cinq gentlemen maudits (The Five Accursed Gentlemen) was filmed in Tunisia. In 1924, Samama-Chikli directed a medium-length film called Ain Al-Ghazal (The Girl from Carthage). The first Tunisian actress was Haydée Chikly, who starred in the short film, Zohra in 1922.

In 1927, the first Tunisian film distribution company, Tunis-Film, started its activities. After independence, movies were exclusively produced by Société Anonyme Tunisienne de Production et d'Expansion Cinématographique (SATPEC). during the 1980s, private production companies and studios emerged. Major foreign movies were filmed in Tunisia including Roman Polanski's Pirates.

In 2007, several films were produced, such as Making Of, directed by Nouri Bouzid and Nejib Belkadi's VHS Kahloucha. In 2013, Abdellatif Kechiche was the first-ever Tunisian director to win the Palme D'Or award. On March 21, 2018, the country opened its first City of Culture. As of March 2018, there were 22 screens all across Tunisia.

Bahrain

The first attempt to establish a cinema in Bahrain was in 1922, by Bahraini businessman Mahmoud Al Saati, who imported a projector and set up a temporary cinema in a hut on the northern coast of Manama.

The first official cinema was established by Abdullah Al-Zayed and his partners in Manama in 1937. The cinema did not have an air-conditioning or heating system, and it is said that the first film was the Egyptian film "Wadad" starring Umm Kulthum.

Bahrain cinema was small because it lacked support from the government and the private sector, as there were many short films produced by individual filmmakers and about five feature films in Bahrain's history. And the Bahrain Cinema and Film Distribution Company began operating in 1967. There are a number of theaters in Bahrain that offer a mixture of Indian, American, and Arab films, and a number of modern-style cinemas in Bahrain, including the 20-screen complex in Bahrain City Centre. Bahrain also has a movie club founded in 1980, and the Bahrain Film Production Company, which was established in 2006 to support the Bahraini film industry.
